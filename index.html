WHAT DO YOU SEE?



What do you see when you close your eyes? What do you see? Undoubtedly, different things. I think you mainly see the space. But I also do not rule out the many things that are in the space, creating a kind of dialogue between the institution that is also the building and the history of this space that is inscribed in these spaces, and which are put in motion by essentially your own memory. They all eventually become just anecdotes-a kind of intangible archive, which runs parallel to the physical archive.


How do you create structure from the chaos of a collection of objects, documents, and stories?


It is many things that speak at the same time. It is the building that speaks, as if the floor cracks open in front of your feet.


The building houses all that has happened. Behind the many layers of paint and plaster, behind the broken and rebuilt walls, under the floors, the dust in the corners...you can excavate.


What does it mean that you show things again? I would say, that we try to see through the building, the cracks in the walls, the leftovers, and see if we can establish relationships between things connected, juxtaposed, or implicated by each other that make them appear as a sort of configuration. Not to capture or nail down the history. It is a tool that provides insight into the way in which we have worked with artists.


All those sketches and discussions about production also give insight into the decision-making process. It is not only about the results- the exhibition, the printed book-but about those processes that demonstrate that a curator, artist, assistant, public enters a room at one point and says: I can see this space, this site, this work, this letter," in a different light now.


But this whole idea of accumulating everything, of establishing a sort of general archive, and making some sort of project of organizing everything and fixing it...is bound to fail.


So you will have to accept that what we have done and what we have now gathered, here, is defined by relationships between things, people, and proximities. And that is defined by its place, here, in this city. Which could be any city. But it is here.


You may ask yourselves how does this institute relate to the world, to the city? What kind of projects have artists developed about this relationship? How have they probed into the city, and how have they opened the view on political "hot potatoes"?


Especially in the early years, we interfered in the city. That had to do with the choice of directors, the way they dealt with the world, what they brought in, and how they then related the local to the global. In the first six years, there had been numerous projects that were connected to the city and engaged with the world. Many contacts and collaborations were activated. It was about making contact and bringing things from there to here and out again.


What was happening in this city at the time-politics, for example-also played a role in our exhibitions.


This city is not just a small city in a small country. It is also a harbor city, a global city, with issues beyond its physical borders... So if you move into the deeper layers of city you could see this city but also other cities, through the lenses of politics, globalism, economy, colonialism, labor.


Making the connection with the city means reaching out to it. We did a lot outside our walls. During this period, it was totally chaotic, and one could say that the city council was experimenting a little-or a lot. To find out where the city had to go, they had try things out. They tested how to make that city... and we did as well.


There are constant dynamics with the city. It always has a certain desire, or impulse, to direct the institute. How desperately the city tries-because we are a public institution and the money comes from there-to push this institute in a certain direction.


There is often a direct line to local areas and sites. Local things that we addressed. But, of course, projects are often a lens through which you look. And you can see how a city is changing. The archive shows us this. When John Knight first came here, this street was where prostitutes gathered. Around the corner was a strip club. And next door was a brothel with twelve air conditioners hanging on the facade because it was so hot inside. Those ladies inside, they needed fresh air in such a space. So in the 1980s nothing was here, and this was also one of the reasons that this location was chosen, to form a connection between parts of the city that seemed to be lost. It worked.


Every show began as an experiment. What we now call a contemporary art center was actually, when we started, a yearlong experiment. It was supposed to last one year. But after that year it continued.


All those remnants of exhibitions. Where is the basketball?! Guillaume Bijl's autographed basketball is a bit wrinkled now, but it has been here for twenty-five years.


There's also a stick from Jessica Stockholder. We have saved a lot, especially from this period. We have thrown away many things as well. Faxes were on thermal paper, so they disappear. There are things you would say now, "Why did we ever throw that away?" Contacts, letters, instructions of artists, all gone.


I'm go A lot disappears in the digital age. Today, many people have a clean desk policy. They want to throw everything away. So since around 2000, when we went digital, we have much less material.


And All that is never thrown away is put into a box. Some people here were very organized. But others have three or four drawers with stuff. But what is the added value of an archive? Everything is in your head, but if people want to investigate something, they need to be able to find out more about the processes and context. Are there still letters? Some people wrote everything on napkins.


An archive is not a fetish. What can be archived depends on who is looking. Are those napkins interesting? Maybe not for one but for the other yes because they really say something about the working process. And that tells another story about an exhibition, people, and relationships. Correspondences with others, certain decisions...That provides a very good insight into how creative processes operate, but also shows the practical, everyday character of an institute.


If you want to tell your story in a certain way, then making specific selections is essential, and it is also important to place your archive within an institution that knows how to deal with it. How you look at the material and how to archive also determines where you can bring it. For example, there is an A4 sheet with a discussion about artist's fees. And this is very much an issue today. "Should artists, or should they not, receive fees?" "Is this important?" "How much?"




SCARS



The whole building is marked by scars, from work, from people... All these ghosts still swarm around, conversing with all the other works that came after them.


And that began with the exhibitions by Guillaume Bijl and John Knight. Thinking through the entire program, I know exactly where they are, these scars.


I can give you ten examples on the second floor. It starts when you enter: the floor cracks there, as if you are walking on ice. That is because of Guillaume Bijl's installation. Because...we were in a hurry, and the installation of that show had to happen while we were still laying the floor, and since then that floor has never been right. Nobody knows it's there, but it is.


The second scar I can identify too. John Knight was there in that room at the left, on the street-facing side of the building, and he had a work there that went across the room diagonally. Ten spirit levels. We had bought some of them, but two belonged to my father. We had taped them to the floor with double-sided tape, and then, at the opening, a visitor taps his foot against one level, and that tape was so awfully strong that this little kick made a big hole in the floor. One big piece snapped off, creating a scar.


Jessica Stockholder created a huge work with bathtubs. And one of those bathtubs simply had to go through a wall. Look, here in this wall. With floodlighting, you can still see the holes it made.


In these walls, in their cracks and crevices, is the real archive.


Jessica Stockholder may have left the building, but over there is a part of her, hidden in the wall.


Herman Pitz had done something with the electrical cables that are normally hidden inside the walls. He wanted to expose the cables to the exterior and for us to remove all the stucco so the viewer would see the enclosed power lines-that was part of his piece. Now we have placed a wall in front of this, but it's still there.


Walls are now in front of that rear facade, and they all cover the windows as well. The heaters are behind there too, so the whole wall is heated.


There, a swing was once hung from the ceiling. You can still see it. It was a work by Irene Fortuyn. You could swing up and down in the space, and while doing this, you would see the windows of the houses at the other end of the courtyard. She asked all the residents to hang flower containers with red flowers from their windowsills. The flowers are gone now, but I still see them if I think of this.


This floor alone you can already analyze in this way. Is this fiction? Or fact? If there's no longer anything in that space is the story still unfolding? How does the memory work? What does it produce? Memory creates, through telling a story as I do now, a chain of events that may become tradition. And that passes on from generation to generation.


In the room, front left, where John Knight's work caused that hole in the floor, Ludger Gerdes made his paintings, in thick oil paint, directly on the wall, and when I say thick, I mean really thick. Perhaps five centimetres thick. At the end of the exhibition, the work had to go. These big pimples of paint were partly removed, as much as we could. We repaired the wall with a top layer of primer, which was then smoothly plastered, but underneath, Ludger's work is still there.


So then, at some point-quite a few years later-a young artist named Brian Jungen arrived, and he wanted to create a sort of cave art, right on that wall. With a grinder, he made these almost primitive drawings on the wall, milled into the plaster. That was a hell of a job because it was the same wall as Ludger Gerdes's painting, and if you are milling into old oil paint, you get a kind of jawbreaker effect.


So in that space you could see, very beautifully, those old layers of Ludger Gerdes. Archaeology, but also a kind of imperceptible archive. Rita McBride also grinned holes, but in the floor, laying bare the layers underneath the linoleum, right through to the concrete. Those floors had been there for a long time. People walked over it continuously, and it looked like traces of the many footsteps of those who had set foot in this building. But they were not-it was a constructed archaeology. The traces gone now, covered by a new floor.


You can see the leftovers of an object or part of an artwork, but you may not always understand its context. If I tell you these stories, they start to unfold and assume a life of their own. So then, suddenly, there is something to see. In that sense, I think that space is never empty.


Of course, this is all mere speculation and all very imaginative.


Billy Apple® wanted the kitchen and workshop removed, to bring the space back to its original state. So we did and then recreated all the of the building's old traces. Everything had to look so sleek! This building is an old school from 1874-where all sorts of things happened. Then-and this is very unusual-we meticulously closed all the holes and eliminated the cracks in this old building. If you look carefully, you will see these differences that otherwise go unnoticed.


This is a house of stories...


To detail all these stories is to begin to imagine how one form is overlaid by what succeeds it, while leaving traces and scars in layers on the body of the building, on its experience.


Maria Anna de Wees is a German artist. We had, at one time, artist editions that remained in the building. And there, on the two end walls, she had made two eyes. The two eyes are still there, located exactly at eye height. Two eyes embedded in the stucco, you can still see them if you look carefully. If we uncover them they would be able to see us again...




CLOUDS



Witte de With, the namesake of the institution and the street it sits on, was a naval hero. The first years were stormy. The street was not what it is now. There were nightclubs and barflies and the city wanted to do basically anything to change this.


So we started thinking from these given facts-the naval hero, the sea, and the clouds. And rain. The cloud is a cumulus cloud. From this you can expect everything: rain, hail, art, and what not.


Initially, there was only the cloud, and in its first appearance, the cloud was more important than the context. But it is a symbol. It is also a form, it is a very powerful figure. One cannot deny that it is a cloud-something that would exist only temporarily, because Witte de With would be a temporary experiment.


A cloud, airy but black.


Black. A black cloud is ominous. And art is ominous.


Such a black cloud... one does not know what happens. That is strong.


It is something "uncanny." The shape of things to come, a cloud hanging.


Witte de With was something completely new, with a very different potential than art spaces that already existed, so it had to be different. The cloud has helped. It is not the cause, but it did help.


It is a public institution, but it was certainly in the early years also a network situation in which art was a private matter and the "funding" was as well. A lot of collectors flocked to the openings. Gifts from collectors were easily given, works were lent, and a type of game was happening, continuously, to push artists to the front, who would then do better on the market. Because if an artist has a large exhibition at "Witte de With" then, of course, the price of the work rises.


The first identity of the cloud remained virtually unchanged until 2004, so it had functioned for ten years.


The newest iteration of cloud is downright bad. No more threat from this form, no rain, no hail, no storm. It's too sweet. It is neither beautiful nor ugly. But it does not have to be because the institution itself has changed.


What is the substantive reason for a cuddly cloud?


When the first cloud was developed, web culture did not quite exist yet. This new cloud strongly recalls the cloud of Apple, the iCloud.



The first sketches imagined the cloud of the weather forecast. Eventually it evolved into the cumulus form: high, menacing, and flattened at the top.


It was a cloud. And with that old black cloud, one can interpret the image directly. It is a cloud, it is not a sheep, and it's not a turd. It is a cloud.


The first cloud is really the thundercloud.


We looked for different ways we could give it new life. Different forms.


If you want to develop identity further then you should cherish the essence that is recognized. You should not throw that overboard. "We have the cloud, we should not move away from it."


So we "saved" the original cloud, but brought in... the notion of development as a dynamic concept. It can be a summer cloud... but it can also be a very dark heavy cloud.


We finally arrived at a cloud that was already there, and we understood it in such a way that it is to a certain extent what the institution stands for. It stands for the center, it stands for the place, for the people who work there. We were looking for a logic to the cloud in such a way that it would always be different. An image that is continuously changing but remains the same. An image that contains a constant recognizability.


There are many interpretations about what the cloud could suggest.


In pictures of sea battles of Witte de With, you see ships with cannons and clouds in the background. The cloud could also stand for explosive ideas.


We never thought about giving it new meaning. We made use of the original. It is a very strong image. It is subtle and silent; it is intellectual and theoretical. It becomes a kind of protest. Activist.


It was important that the cloud returned in a new disguise. In whatever form.


What is a cloud? A...dream?!


Will this cloud just hover? Drift? Where are irs borders?


Art is never one vision. There are always pluralities and multiple insights. If you put all the shapes on top of each other, you get a very different picture.


At that point, the changeover, the shift, that took place was related to the program and the exhibition. That was where the overlapping of the different clouds came from.



For each part of the program layers were superimposed. So the cloud changed again and again and again.


It reflected how the institution worked. In the beginning, we did not know what the program would be.


The plurality, the variations of the cloud were also how the program developed thereafter. Multiple directions.


Does the cloud come first, and then the program, or does the program generate the cloud?


Drawing... going back to the line. Away from the heaviness, to something that can be continuously redefined. At a certain point, it was only one line. And from there we continued to analyze forms to find the appearance of a cloud.


Anyone can draw a cloud. And because anyone can draw a cloud, you can play with the idea that there really is an archetype, a form that can be thought of by anyone. When layering all these over each other, new visions of a cloud come together as a new form.


The first cloud is firm.


Look at the clouds that follow: they are fragile, or vulnerable, or variable. This overlaying, layer upon layer, is creating a form of archaeology.


The choice to make the cloud eventually transparent is, of course, an important difference compared to the black cloud. It relates to the desire for transparency. In politics, in society, there was an urgent call for transparency, so that everybody would understand what something was. So that everyone could understand what art is, what was going on, who everybody else is. But transparency does not exist. Saying that everything should be clear, visible, for everyone to see, this creates a false sense of understanding, of access, of visibility. And so these new series of clouds reflect what was going on. False transparency, confusion, plurality.


The latest cloud is friendly, easier, and more accessible.


The latest cloud wants to be "playful." But the playfulness is a misunderstanding.


It may look sweet, accessible, recognizable, but it is more corporate. Even if very accessible, it is a super-stylized symbol. A standard sign. An iconic symbol. The simplest design you can make. So that is why the playfulness is false. As with the sweet, funny icons in our smartphones: they look friendly, like something to play with, but behind these super cute symbols are not-so-cute corporations.


There should be multiple clouds again after this. So that you are going to review the institution again after a few years because the identity has changed, and continues to change.


To see what works. If it still works. If it still fits.


Examining the evolution of the logo could be understood as "institutional critique." First, there was the basic black cloud, and then several variations of that first cloud appeared. Then, in the third iteration, it became important keep the cloud as a logo, as a motive.


We approached the cloud as something continuous, something that could I grow and change. Sometimes it is good that it is more an evolution than a completely different entity.


Looking at the original cloud, and then at the next stage of the design, we saw a lot of confusion. It was no longer easily recognizable as a cloud. It looked more like a very rainy day.


Our idea was a cloud as a group of things. A group of people, a group of artists, a variation of politics and layers. To create more dimensions.


We made a radical translation of the first cloud. A simplified version. In the 1990s, communication happened in a different way than it does now. We could not travel that quickly, "online" did not exist. These changes in communication needed to be reflected in an update of the cloud. It is more about visual identity now than it was back in those days.


It is very important to see the cloud grow and take on a life of its own, to add new chapters to its story.


When one is drawing clouds, it is crucial to understand what makes them recognizable as a cloud or not. To visualize, one must start sketching. We did pages and pages of clouds, in ink. Thought clouds, or like clouds of gas, rather than a cloud in the sky.


The cloud has moved from an almost completely autonomous logo to a much more accessible and friendly cloud.


The first cloud was a terrifying one, especially the full black version. How the logo evolved runs parallel to the cultural climate in the arts and to how this institution is forced to meet the expectations of more transparency, to be more audience-friendly.


The changes in the cloud illustrate that change of climate and that change of expectations.

Tracing the Past, Shaping the Future: Bik Van der Pol's Experimental Reflection on Rotterdam's Art and Witte de With Exhibition.



Following our twenty-fifth anniversary, I invited Bik Van der Pol to work with us on creating a temporary institute-one that would bring in and reimagine perspectives on the city of Rotterdam through Witte de With and vice versa. It would be born from their practice and their observations of Witte de With's exhibition history and her evolution within the political context of the city, and, unlike the many other museums in Rotterdam, it would not omit the lineage of critically engaged, international contemporary artistic practices from the (art) history of the city-a task that I felt could only be entrusted to them.


Rijksmuseum-in-rebuilding1Bik Van der Pol Fly Me To The Moon. Rijksmuseum Amsterdam (6 October-19 November 2006). Bik Van der Pol had watched Witte de With develop since its inception in 1990 with every new director's arrival in Rotterdam. They paid close attention to the socioeconomic and political contexts in which the institution operated. They would be the perfect interlocutors for weaving and interfacing inbetween, drawing on resources from both in and out of the institution's walls.


On our end, an institutional spring-cleaning was in order; no one had yet dared to dig into our drawers and servers. As such, we set a growing collection in motion, tracing previously unorganized in-house materials while hoping to honor their extended networks at and around Witte de With. First, we identified in our storage project-related leftovers from our rich exhibition history. We then tread slowly through accumulations of primary source material, such as correspondences, artists' documents, and sketches from artists, curators, and installation team By inviting artists to enact an institutional imagination exercise with and Through our institution, our desire was not only to work together to catalogue what might otherwise become lost in both the history of the institution and art's history in chwhere the institution is located but also to embark on a survey with Bik Van of the politics of accumulation-through a possible exposé of the results of the sincere and honest research.


Bik Van der Pol agreed and had carte blanche to follow their own drives and urges. They interviewed artists, staff, and graphic designers. They pored over our bookshelves and boxes, rified through our basement, and uncovered the cracks in our 140-year-old building/They worked with our team dedicated to their research, a process they approached from myriad angles, with various methods and steady progress. They spoke to anyone with whom they wished about the institute's history and did so as subjectively as possible. Theirs was a rather "global" endeavor. In exchange, the institution would serve as the vessel for their memories and private conversations, which were distilled into a script that informed boin the exhibition and this publication. Lastly, they wished to assemble fragments and traces of artworks related to their memory and to our deputy director's twenty-five years of stories They would approach this endeavor as a site-sensitive preject, diving into exhibitions and initiatives that were otherwise unlikely to be re-visited as the issues and urgencies they negotiated have disappeared from the city's physical and temporal landscapes. They hunted down seemingly on-the-fringes material- support structures, plans, notes, stories, and witness reports-and were not afraid to equate these remnants with artworks. So in their words, "document" became "art" and "art" became "source material." Theirs was an exercise in collecting as well as in staging-though by no means pornographic. It would be an anti-exhibition statement where evidence was laid bare but with a careful resistance. They surveyed the institution's physical space and embarked on countless dialogues with its exhibition history, in turn re-activating the work of many artists but also the many methods those artists had undertaken over the decades. I will refrain from listing here the many artists, articles, documents, or works given the breadth of traces, fragments, and correspondences revealed. You have only to turn to the inventory to appreciate the wide scope.


This exhibition serves as a poignant case study and a baseline for-if not an introduction to-a remarkable chapter in history: the intellectual expansion of the 1980s and its unfolding through the 1990s to the early 2000s. Bik Van der Pol's emphasis on the first decade of Witte de With's existence not only drew from the comfort of the distance of time but also reinvigorated the palpable connection to the local through the problems artists of that era confronted, be it drugs, prostitution, or the city's economic dependency on the harbor and the European Union. Then, Rotterdam's complications were all on display, extremely visible, and not as structurally imbedded, politically imposed, or seemingly imperceptible as they are today. Or, let us now ask, were they really so visible?


It makes the following attempts:

1. To unpack the tension between the contemporary and the record.
2. To consider the "collection and display of traces" experiment from a broader point of view of contemporary art (and its discontents) and to offer a critical take on the methodology of the equal treatment of art, trace, and document.
3. To treat this experiment not only as a manifestation of a symptom of the auto-immune disorders of a contemporary art institute per se but as a global phenomenon with a historical trajectory across disciplines, with a focus on this moment in time.
4. To inform, as the institution embarks on its next chapter, from 2018 onward.

You often work with preexisting material and contexts and settings that call for a response, an interaction, or an intervention. Is your approach inspired by a suspicion of the obsession with the "new"?

Since we began working together, we have been very much aware of not adding to this stream of the "new." We would rather engage with some sort of reorganization of what already is-selecting something from the stream of things and through a process of unfolding and unraveling, unlocking hidden information in objects and bodies of knowledges and assumptions. When we begin to work with a situation and its context, we first take an outside perspective. What emerges from such a site-specific and site-sensitive approach? For Photosynthesis, Tue Greenfort asked Witte de With to switch its power supply to an environmentally friendly provider for the duration of his exhibition. Subsequent correspondence with energy supplier ENECO (presented in the exhibition) reveals the consequences involved in such a change. His project explores corporate social responsibility and aspects of value in energy production: "green" energy is technically not worth more than "gray" energy, but its extra costs are due to energy being now a "product" on the free market. The electricity used by Witte de With would remain, in fact, unchanged, but ENECO would buy energy from sustainable sources. After much discussion, it proved to be impossible to switch the institution to green energy for the six weeks of the exhibition, but for 2007, ENECO offered to make the switch and reimburse Witte de With for its extra costs. 1989

Galerie 't Venster closes in advance of the new art center's opening. The new art center will be called Witte de With Center for Contemporary Art.

Renovations begin at Witte de Withstraat 50. The building's second and third floors are made available to the Witte de With Foundation.

Witte de With's original logo is designed by the graphic design group Hard Werken. Posters of a cloud appear around Rotterdam. The only text that accompanies the image is "Expected 27 January."


1990

Witte de With Center for Contempo- rary Art opens 27 January.

The Rotterdam city government begins a campaign to rid the Witte de Withstraat of its seedy reputation. Various cultural institutions are placed "in the area to create the "Axis of Art," a major urban renewal project. Ken Lum pastes poster of his artwork Melly Shum Hates Her Job around Rotterdam. The poster on the sidewall of the Witte de With building becomes a permanent billboard.


1992

Kunsthal Rotterdam opens in the Museumpark.


1993

Nederlands Architectuurinstituut (NAi) opens in the Museumpark.


1996

During the digital project Downtime by Tony Brown, Witte de With launches its first website: www.wdw.nl.


1997

The Witte de With Foundation becomes the owner of Witte de Withstraat 50, with Rotterdamse Hogeschool as a tenant on the ground floor.


1998

Rotterdamse Hogeschool relocates. Witte de With temporarily takes over the space for offices.


1999

TENT, the exhibition space of the Centrum voor Beeldende Kunst Rotterdam, opens on the ground floor of Witte de Withstraat 50 on 19 September.

Arienne Boelens and Maurits de Bruijn design a new website for Witte de With.


2004

A new Witte de With website designed by Petra Dreiskampfer, Nick Koning. Matthe Stet, and Wijbrand Stet Matthe Stet, and Wijbrand Stet launches.


2006

Witte de With renovates its exhibition spaces, changing the gallery floors from pale blue to dark gray and adding polaroid foil to the third-floor windows.

Kummer & Herrman redesign Hand Werken's original black cloud logo. The new design uses multiple outlines of clouds that change with the season.


2009

Following Billy Apple®'s exhibition Revealed/Concealed, Witte de With rebuilds its second-floor gallery.


2011

Remco van Bladel designs a new website for Witte de With.


2012

A Practice For Everyday Life creates a new logo and graphic identity.


2013

Witte de With renovates the entrance reception area and begins using a ground-floor gallery, which is visible from the street. 2016 Remco van Bladel redesigns Witte de With's website.


2017

Witte de With is challenged, through the process of the Cinema Olanda: Platform, to reconsider the legacies of its name, and comes to examine the origins of its name, drawn in 1990 from the street on which it is situated, and Admiral Witte Corneliszoon de With (1599-1658), a Dutch naval officer engaged in colonial exploits.
